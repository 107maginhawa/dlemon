# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Independent owner-dentists running small practices (1–3 providers, often a single 2-chair clinic). Persona: **Dr. Maya Reyes**, 35, owns a two-chair practice — clinically excellent, business-pragmatic, tired of paying rent on software she'll never own. Daily champion: **Joy**, the office manager who actually lives in the tool (scheduling, billing, records). Secondary: an **associate dentist** ("Dr. Sam") — the "add a dentist" growth story.

Context of use: a prospective buyer evaluating practice-management software, skeptical of SaaS lock-in, doing the mental math on subscription cost over the life of the practice. They arrive wanting to know one thing — *is this actually mine, and will it keep working?*

## Product Purpose

Dentalemon is **local-first, buy-once** dental practice software — records/EMR, billing (payment plans + receivables, e.g. braces), scheduling, and **SUN** (the carousel timeline: a Single Unified Narrative of a patient's history; brand color yellow = sun = lemon). The marketing site exists to sell one promise: **real ownership.** Local-first + buy-once = your software, your data, on your machine, that keeps working even if the company disappears — the trust line a subscription vendor structurally cannot say.

Success = an owner-dentist arrives skeptical of "another dental SaaS," and leaves convinced this is the one product they can actually *own*, and converts (waitlist request or purchase depending on launch model below).

**v1 scope to feature:** SUN, Records/EMR, Billing, Scheduling. Do **not** mention imaging / perio / ceph — those are parked future one-time add-on modules. Do **not** call the product "complete."

## Brand Personality

Movement + premium. Part manifesto, part sterling-quality product. Voice is confident and a little defiant — the underdog economics of ownership against the subscription machine. The villain is **the subscription model itself**, never a named competitor.

Three words: **defiant, premium, trustworthy.**

- Movement refrain: **"Unsubscribe."** (give it an object on big uses — "Unsubscribe from dental software" — never on buy buttons).
- Tagline: **"Buy once. Own forever."**
- Secondary refrain: **"Own your practice."**
- Page rhythm: **provoke → prove → affirm.**

**Go-to-market framing is undecided — hold both, pick the CTA model at build time:**
- *Invite-only* — PH "Founding 100" launch; primary CTA "Request Founding Access" → signup. Hero H1 candidate: "You built the clinic. Own the software that runs it."
- *Direct buy-once* — buy/own now; CTAs "Own it" / "Get Dentalemon." Hero H1 candidate: "Stop renting your practice."

Both serve the same promise; only the conversion mechanic differs.

## Anti-references

- **Generic dental-SaaS marketing** — feature-grid + "trusted by" logos + free-trial CTA. The whole template we're arguing against.
- **Subscription-SaaS pricing-tier pages** ($X/mo, $Y/mo, "Most Popular"). Pricing here is a one-time number, not a recurring ladder.
- **Enterprise-healthcare blandness** — navy/teal, stock smiling-staff photos, "HIPAA-compliant cloud platform" boilerplate.
- **AI-slop landing scaffolding** — tiny uppercase tracked eyebrows above every section, numbered 01/02/03 section markers, identical icon-heading-text card grids, hero-metric template, gradient text.
- Do not overclaim completeness or mention parked modules (imaging/perio/ceph).

## Design Principles

- **Ownership is the whole argument.** Every section should reinforce "this is yours and it keeps working" — the one thing a SaaS can't claim. Lead with the promise a competitor structurally cannot make.
- **Provoke, then prove.** Earn the defiance with concrete proof (real product — SUN carousel on a real screen, not illustration), then affirm. Don't provoke without backing it up.
- **Premium underdog, not cheap underdog.** Buy-once is the value, but the craft must read as sterling. Underdog economics, luxury execution — never bargain-bin.
- **Show the real product.** The SUN timeline is the hero feature; show an actual capture, not a placeholder or abstract diagram.
- **Sell one product, not a feature list.** Four pillars (SUN, Records, Billing, Scheduling) in service of one promise. Resist the comprehensive feature-grid reflex.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA (matches the product app's a11y bar): body text ≥4.5:1 contrast, 44px touch targets, visible focus states. Reduced-motion alternatives required for the manifesto/reveal choreography (the brand invites first-load motion). Diverse, real brand cast in imagery (Dr. Maya — Asian woman; Dr. Sam — male associate; Joy — Black, front desk). No em-dashes in visible marketing copy (prior brand rule — verify in DOM).
